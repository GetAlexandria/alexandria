## The objective

Change SocioTechnica's **economic posture** — get *resourced* — so the founders
can keep building rather than walk away with nothing after working like dogs.
Not unlimited wealth; "Alan Kay money" and a seat at the table. The plan is held
loosely; changing it could be the smartest move.

## The core pitch

One story, tailored per audience: **"imagine if you were doing this with these
guys."** Who we say it to, and the slight reframe for each, is the whole game.

## The check-down pyramid

A flexible ladder of leverage. Three forms of leverage — revenue coming in, an
investor offer, an acquirer offer — get played against each other.

### 1. Acquirers (top of the pyramid right now)

- The frame: "imagine running your company on Alexandria, then selling that
  transformation to the world." Best fit = whoever has the **best distribution
  and is furthest behind on AI**. Distinguish **internal opportunity** (anyone
  could AI-ify themselves) from **external opportunity** (a buyer who can take it
  to a market) — the big offers come from external opportunity. Notion-shaped
  buyers are the head-and-shoulders targets; ~3 companies stood out where there's
  an in and a clear fit.
- The outcome we want: **a couple million dollars each plus well-compensated
  work together**, keep working on this (not contracting). The argument vs.
  shooting for the moon: in a frothy, consolidating market, getting paid now
  beats a speculative moonshot ("in six months no one will SaaS").
- The window: **roughly six months** while the market is frothy and consolidating
  (e.g., Ona → OpenAI). Acquirers exist for ~6 months; clients exist for a decade
  — so take the acquirer swing first.
- Risks / failure modes: a great deal at a bad company, or a great company with a
  bad deal; **due-diligence theft** ("they steal our shit because it's easy
  now"); slow-played or **offensively low offers**, sharpened by the founders
  being in North Carolina and unwilling to relocate permanently.

### 2. Investors (open generally, but NOT a media-plan target)

- We remain **open to investors generally** — but the **media plan does not aim
  at them**, and we don't feel the need to target them through the episodes.
  Investors are pursued deliberately in the **business-development playbook (a
  separate workstream)**. Don't shape media for investors.
- The standing terms if it comes up: ~**$5M at ~$20M** for two years of
  independence, modeled on what Fabro got. The private aim: a fast ~5x to a
  **~$100M sale in under 36 months**, keep control, "build our lab on the lake."
  Board meetings exist but get run by an agent ("they'll love it — these guys are
  so AI").

### 3. Clients / consulting (the sustainable fallback)

- AI-ify companies at high day rates (~**$400–500/hr**, large hour counts);
  available "for the next decade." Going from zero-to-something here **needs no
  demo**.
- Lets the founders be choosy — take clients who actually want Alexandria; reskin
  for them if needed (no weird coins for the client) over the same underlying
  tech. Internally, though: **"we will not remove the coins."**
- The split to watch: selling *our* products (Playmaker Studio, Alexandria) vs.
  just doing generic AI-engineering consulting. Leverage comes partly from money
  in the door and partly from **receipts** ("we installed this at X").

### Client-acquihire hybrid (the weird in-between)

Embed via professional services, trial partnerships, get more integrated in
companies we like — aiming at an **acquihire through client work** rather than
through flashiness. Concrete example: help **Brian grow Fabro's professional-
services side** (not his gift), as a path from the current unsustainable setup to
a real partnership. Every one of these is bespoke and muddy.

## Sequencing and timeline (the current dart)

- **Ship a signal-flare demo in June**, network it **July–August**, close a deal
  **September–October** (before the election / Thanksgiving).
- Order: targeted **acquirers first**; **investors only if it's hot**; ready to
  **pivot to clients within 2–3 weeks** if it's cold (clients are the safe
  floor — "I have a hard time imagining how we could fail" to land consulting in
  a few weeks).
- Distribution motion when the demo drops: email the mailing list, post a lab
  note, cover the bases on Twitter/Bluesky — then targeted outreach off a tiered
  acquirer list / network analysis / CRM. (There's a half-built sales-and-
  marketing Alexandria + outreach agent "on the shelf.")

## What the media has to do

The media paints a credible picture of **living in the future** — *"imagine if
you were doing this with these guys."* It is aimed at **acquirers and clients**
(not investors — see above). It must **show receipts** (real, not faked) —
authenticity is the trust mechanism. Format is a **vignette/episode series**, not
one big demo (see canonical strategy doc). There are many ways to paint "the
future"; the producer's job is to choose the pictures that best serve acquirers
and clients.

## Risk discipline

- The **Next LX failure mode**: over-investing weeks in acquirer/investor
  conversations "to no avail" while the safe client floor goes unbuilt. Hold the
  plan loosely; read the tea leaves; don't sink months chasing cold leads.
- **Human approval gate** on all outreach and sends (AI drafts; humans decide).
- Founders stay in **North Carolina**; relocation price "has gone up." Plan
  around it.
