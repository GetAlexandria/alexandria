# Demo Thesis Process

Demo Thesis is station 1 in Damien's software-product demo process. Its artifact
is a one-page brief that answers: who is this for, what must they believe after
watching, what problem frame opens the video, what value proposition matters
most, and what proof will count as convincing.

## Inputs

- Product positioning, product context, or Raven-banked source material
- Target customer or viewer hypothesis
- Current customer objections
- Competitive context and current alternatives
- Product capabilities and known limits
- Launch, sales, fundraising, recruiting, or internal-alignment goal

## Process

1. Define the target viewer precisely.
2. Define viewer context, pain, sophistication, objections, and current
   alternatives.
3. Choose the category or positioning frame.
4. Write one value proposition, not a feature list.
5. Define the before/after belief change.
6. Identify 3-5 proof requirements.
7. Define success metrics, primary CTA, and "not doing" scope.
8. Stress-test with product truth: remove or weaken unsupported claims.

## Quality Checks

- Would the target viewer recognize themselves?
- Is the belief change specific enough to drive story decisions?
- Is the demo promise singular?
- Are there no more than 3-5 proof requirements?
- Can each proof requirement be shown, sourced, or deliberately explained?
- Could a viewer repeat the core promise after one viewing?
- Does the CTA follow from what the video proves?

## Failure Modes

| Failure | What it looks like | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Feature soup | The thesis lists capabilities instead of a belief change. | Force one value proposition plus 3-5 proof points. |
| Audience blur | The same video tries to serve buyer, admin, evaluator, and user. | Pick one primary viewer or split the demo. |
| No proof burden | Strong claims appear before checking whether the product can show them. | Add a proof-required field for every claim. |
| Copy-first vanity | The team debates lines before agreeing on positioning. | Freeze thesis before Story Spine. |
| No external reality | The brief sounds good internally but has no customer grounding. | Mark assumptions and collect source material. |

## Done When

You can say: "After watching, this viewer should believe ___ strongly enough to
___," and every proof requirement is either visibly demoable, source-backed, or
intentionally out of scope.
